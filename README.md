Project overview and objective: 
This project focuses on building a regression-based machine learning system to forecast 
the number of seats likely to be sold per ride. The goal is to help transportation providers 
better manage capacity, reduce seat wastage, and optimize pricing and scheduling 
decisions. 
Objective: 
● Better fleet and seat allocation 
● Reduced overbooking and underutilization 
● Improved operational efficiency 
● Visualising the data in the form of graphs for better insights 
● Data-driven decision-making for transport planning

Workflow of the code: 
The process starts by importing required libraries for data processing, modeling, 
evaluation, and visualization. It then loads the dataset ‘train_final.csv’ and groups it at the 
ride level using ‘ride_id’, converting raw passenger-level records into aggregated 
ride-level features such as passenger count, route details, timing, fare, demand behavior, 
and booking patterns. 
Next, new engineered features are created like time format conversions, route/slot 
identifiers, interaction variables (e.g., fuel × distance), urgency metrics, and 
holiday/weekend combinations. Categorical variables like route and origin are encoded 
into numeric form using label encoding. 
The dataset is then split into training and testing sets, followed by a more advanced step: 
K-Fold smoothed mean encoding, which replaces categorical values with statistically 
smoothed target means to improve predictive power while reducing overfitting. 
After that, additional interaction features are generated by combining important variables 
(like demand × time, fare × distance, lead time × slot, etc.), and the final feature set is 
prepared for modeling. 
The code then trains a wide range of machine learning models including: 
● Linear models (Linear Regression, Ridge, Lasso, ElasticNet, Huber) 
● Distance-based models (KNN, SVR) 
● Tree models (Decision Tree) 
● Ensemble models (Random Forest, Bagging, Extra Trees) 
● Boosting models (GBM, AdaBoost, XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost) 
Each model is evaluated using R², MAE, and RMSE, and for boosting/ensemble models, 
performance is tracked across training iterations (“epochs” or trees) using staged 
predictions or warm-start checkpoints. 
All predictions are stored and compared, and a final ranking of models is produced based 
on R² score. 
Finally, the code generates multiple visualizations: 
● Predicted vs Actual plots for all models 
● Model comparison charts (R², MAE, MSE, ranking) 
● Learning curves for boosting/ensemble models 
The feature importance section standardizes feature names, categorizes them (raw, 
booking, interaction, mean-encoded), computes importance scores, and plots them in a 
clean multi-model dashboard.

Key insights from this project: 
1. Demand is highly time-dependent 
Passenger demand varies significantly with hour, day of the week, and month. Peak hours 
such as morning and evening consistently show higher bookings, indicating strong 
commuter travel patterns. 
2. Weekends and holidays significantly affect travel behavior 
Features like ‘is_weekend’, ‘is_holiday’, and ‘is_school_hol’ show that demand changes 
noticeably during non-working days. Weekend travel is generally less predictable and 
often reflects leisure-driven patterns. 
3. Route and origin are major demand drivers 
Encoded features such as route, travel_from, and slot mean encoding are among the most 
important predictors. This shows that location-specific travel behavior plays a major role 
in demand variation. 
4. Booking behavior strongly influences demand prediction 
Variables such as booking urgency, ticket lead time, and lead spread significantly impact 
predictions. Shorter booking lead times are often associated with higher demand 
fluctuations and urgency-based travel. 
5. Ensemble and boosting models perform best 
Models like XGBoost, LightGBM, Random Forest, and CatBoost consistently 
outperform linear and distance-based models. This confirms that demand patterns are 
highly non-linear and involve complex feature interactions. 
6. Feature engineering significantly improves performance 
Interaction features such as fuel × distance, slot × week, and lead × holiday improve 
model accuracy. This shows that demand is influenced more by combined effects of 
variables rather than individual features. 
7. Mean encoding captures hidden demand patterns 
Smoothed target encoding features such as slot_mean, route_mean, and origin_mean help 
capture historical demand behavior effectively. These features contribute significantly to 
model performance improvements. 
8. External factors also influence demand 
Variables like fuel price, rainy season, distance, and competition levels affect travel 
demand, though their impact is smaller compared to time and route-based features. 
9. Model agreement improves reliability 
Top-performing models produce relatively consistent predictions, indicating stable 
learning patterns and strong underlying relationships in the dataset. 
10. Overall conclusion 
Passenger demand is best explained by a combination of time-based patterns, route 
characteristics, booking behavior, and seasonal effects. No single feature dominates; 
instead, demand is driven by interactions between multiple factors.
